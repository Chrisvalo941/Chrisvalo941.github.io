Features of the Animal Kingdom

Scientists predict that only 15% of species are known to man from the estimated 8.7 million species. That leaves almost 85% species yet to be discovered. Only about 953,434 species of animals have been catalogued in the Animal Kingdom.

The Animal Kingdom, also known as Kingdom Animalia, is one of the major classifications in biological taxonomy, grouping together all living organisms that are considered animals. This kingdom is distinct from other life forms like plants, fungi, and microorganisms, and includes an incredibly diverse range of species, from tiny insects to massive whales.

Despite the vast diversity within the animal kingdom, all animals share certain common traits that set them apart from organisms in other kingdoms. Animals are eukaryotic and multicellular, with most possessing specialized tissues. They are also generally motile, at least during certain stages of their life cycle. Unlike autotrophs such as plants, which make their own food through photosynthesis, or fungi, which break down food outside their bodies, all animals are heterotrophic. This means they must consume living or dead organic matter for energy. Animals can be grouped based on their diets into carnivores (meat-eaters), herbivores (plant-eaters), omnivores (eating both plants and animals), or parasites (organisms that feed off others).
